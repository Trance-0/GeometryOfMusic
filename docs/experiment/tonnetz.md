## What it is, in music-theory terms

The **Tonnetz** (German for "tone network") is the classical diagram
that lays out the twelve pitch classes on a triangular lattice so that

- each horizontal step is a perfect fifth,
- each upward-right step is a major third,
- each upward-left step is a minor third.

Every small triangle in that lattice is a major or minor triad. The
**neo-Riemannian transformations** — P (Parallel), L (Leading-tone
exchange), and R (Relative) — are the three ways to flip a triangle
across one of its edges:

- **P** swaps the triad between major and minor (C ↔ Cm).
- **L** exchanges the root for its leading tone (C ↔ Em).
- **R** moves to the relative minor / major (C ↔ Am).

Any progression built from smooth voice leading can be read as a walk
on this graph of triangles. Richard Cohn's *Audacious Euphony* and
Tymoczko's *A Geometry of Music* treat the Tonnetz as a practical
analytical tool for 19th-century chromatic music (Wagner, Liszt,
Schubert).

## What it is, in mathematical terms

The Tonnetz tessellates the infinite Euclidean plane with equilateral
triangles, but the underlying pitch-class system is periodic in all
three direction: `+3` semitones, `+4` semitones, `+7` semitones all
return to the same pitch class mod 12. Quotienting by those three
generators leaves a **torus** whose triangulation has 12 vertices
(pitch classes) and 24 triangles (12 major + 12 minor triads). The
P / L / R operations are reflections across the shared edges.

Formally, the Tonnetz is the 1-skeleton of a triangulation of the
torus `T² = R² / L` where `L` is the lattice generated by
`(3, 4, 7)`-periodic shifts.

## What this experiment will render

- 12 pitch-class nodes arranged on a toroidally-tiled triangular grid.
- 24 triads, colored by mode (major / minor).
- P, L, R edges in distinct colors so a progression colors-in the
